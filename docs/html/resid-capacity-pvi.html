Capacity-matched leakage
— the independent token-id reader

An attack-independent probe predicts the token behind a hidden state without ever reading the embedding table. Reducing its feature dimension below the validation count removes a catastrophic estimator failure and yields a reader whose accuracy tracks an embedding-inversion attack at correlation 0.80 to 1.00 under representation-space defenses. Under input-layer differential privacy the two diverge with depth, and the divergence localizes what the defense protects.

research report · 2026-06-23
§01

Introduction

problem and headline finding

A leakage probe is useful only if it is independent of the attack it is meant to predict. A reader that recovers the token identity from a hidden state, and never consults the embedding table, is independent by construction. The question is whether such a reader can be made a well-behaved estimator, and whether it then predicts a real inversion attack.

The threat model is an adversary that knows the model weights, the embedding table, and any published defense parameters, and can synthesize unlimited pairs of defended representation and token. The secret is the token identity behind a residual-stream vector in gemma-2-2b (dimension 2304, an evaluation pool of 2048 candidate tokens).

The token-identity reader (here called the capacity-matched probe) originally failed as a bits estimator: with more features than validation points it interpolates, and its information content evaluated on a label-shuffled control falls to roughly negative fifty bits at every depth, an artifact rather than a leakage signal. Reducing the feature dimension below the validation count bounds that control value near negative one bit at every depth, at about 0.57 times the cost. The repaired reader's bounded top-1 accuracy then tracks the inversion attack's recovery rate at Spearman correlation 0.80 to 1.00 under representation-space defenses, while under input-layer differential privacy the two attenuate and finally diverge as the observation layer deepens.

§03

Method

measurement procedure

For each surface and defense strength, two independent computations ran on the same hidden states. The attack fit a ridge map to the embedding and decoded the nearest token, scored by exact-match recovery rate on held-out tokens. The probe standardized the hidden state on training statistics, projected to a fixed dimension below the validation count by covariance eigendecomposition, fit a calibrated linear-softmax reader over token-identity classes, and reported both the reader accuracy and the information selectivity against a label-shuffled control. Spearman correlation between the probe readout and the attack recovery was then computed across the defense sweep, separately at each observation layer.

The hidden state feeds two independent paths. The attack reconstructs the embedding and decodes the nearest token; the probe predicts the token identity from a dimension-reduced reader that never touches the embedding table. Their agreement across a defense sweep is the test of whether the probe predicts the attack.

§04

Leakage Measures

two independent probes

Token-identity reader. Predicts the token-identity class from the hidden state. It is independent of the inversion attack because it never reads the embedding table and predicts a class label, not a vector; its correlation with a retrieval-style reader is 0.66 to 0.76, below the redundancy threshold. The single change from the original failing version is the dimensionality reduction, which adds no attack information. Two readouts: bounded reader accuracy (primary) and predictive information in bits (auxiliary).

Channel mutual information. A variational upper bound on the mutual information between hidden state and token, in bits, computed by a separate estimator. It is reported beside the reader to show that any depth effect is a property of the signal rather than of one estimator.

§06

Findings

claims as paper statements

Capacity matching repairs the independent token-identity probe. The original reader failed as a bits estimator because it had more features than validation points and interpolated; on a label-shuffled control its information fell to roughly negative fifty bits at every depth. Reducing the feature dimension below the validation count bounds that control value near negative one bit at every depth, at about 0.57 times the cost, with the repair driven by the dimension reduction rather than by the regularization strength. The repaired probe's bounded reader accuracy tracks the inversion attack at correlation 0.80 to 1.00 under representation-space defenses. Supported for gemma-2-2b; the accuracy readout is the object that tracks, the bits readout is auxiliary. Generality across models and seeds is not yet established.

The probe and the attack decouple with depth under input-layer privacy. As the observation layer deepens, the correlation between reader accuracy and attack recovery attenuates from +0.96 to −0.21, turning negative at layer 20. The independent channel-information estimator attenuates in the same direction, from +0.96 to +0.29, but stays positive: it does not reproduce the sign reversal. The shared attenuation indicates a property of the propagated signal; the sign reversal at layer 20 is specific to the bounded token-identity readout. This is single-seed and not yet robust.

§07

Analysis

why the estimator fails and how it is bounded

The repair has a proof. The probe's information in bits is the log-probability the reader assigns the true token minus the prior log-probability. Because a probability can approach zero, this quantity has no lower bound over readers: a single confidently-wrong assignment sends it to negative infinity. Accuracy, an indicator average, stays in the unit interval. This is why accuracy cannot blow up while the bits can (Lemma 1).

With more features than validation points the data is separable, and an unregularized log-loss fit interpolates: training probabilities approach one. On a shuffled control the confident prediction disagrees with the permuted label on at least one point, the assigned probability there approaches zero, and the empirical control information, a finite average with one diverging term, falls without bound. This is the mechanism of the observed floor, not a derivation of its exact value (Proposition 2).

If the reader's logits are bounded by a cap fixed independently of the feature dimension, then every class probability is at least a positive softmax floor, so the information is bounded below by a finite, dimension-independent constant (Proposition 3). Dimensionality reduction does not enter this bound directly; it is the practical device that keeps the fit out of the interpolation regime so a modest logit cap is attainable, which is why the repair tracks the dimension rather than the regularization strength. The full proof is in the claim record.

§08

Discussion

what leaks, and the limit

Under representation-space defenses the independent probe and the inversion attack agree closely at every depth: the probe predicts the attack. Under input-layer privacy they diverge as the noise propagates through the network, and the divergence localizes what the defense protects. Input-layer privacy degrades the embedding geometry that the attack reconstructs before it degrades the token-identity decodability that the probe measures; at the deepest layer the embedding reconstruction has collapsed while the token identity remains partly decodable, so the two measures move oppositely. Because an independent information estimator attenuates in parallel under propagated privacy (though it does not itself reverse sign), this is a property of the propagated signal rather than an artifact of the probe.

Two scope notes follow from the design. The agreement reported here is intervention-ordered co-tracking: a defense parameter degrades several readouts in the same order, so a high rank correlation establishes co-movement under ordered intervention, not mechanistic or causal equivalence. The independence of probe and attack rules out the probe reading the attack's own output, but not shared dependence on representation geometry, token frequency, or layerwise separability.

Limitations and recorded negatives. The results are a single model and a single seed, with seven privacy points per layer and no confidence intervals. A leave-one-out check on the input-privacy sweep gives a robustness floor short of multiple seeds: removing any single point, the correlation stays positive at layer 12 (range +0.09 to +0.60) and stays negative at layer 20 (range −0.94 to −0.09), so the layer-20 sign survives single-point deletion while its magnitude remains volatile. The depth reversal is reported as an observation in this run, not an established effect. The information-in-bits readout is calibration-sensitive and is reported only as auxiliary. The Gaussian class-conditional reader remains miscalibrated even after dimensionality reduction, so capacity matching is necessary but not sufficient: the reader must also be calibrated. Named next steps are multiple seeds with intervals, cross-model replication, a sensitivity check at a smaller reduced dimension, a calibration diagnostic on the bits-fragile cells, and a matched embedding-cosine readout at the deepest layer to confirm that geometry collapses while identity decodability survives.
