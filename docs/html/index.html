Confidential-inference leakage
— a measurement map

An information-theoretic, interpretability-grounded study of attacks on transformer inference. The method is one loop: measure how invertible a released representation is with an attack-independent probe (in bits), run a recovery attack on the same representation, then test whether the bits predict the recovery across a sweep from plaintext through a defense parameter. Where bits and recovery correlate, the probe predicts the attack; where they diverge, that divergence is the finding. Each surface below carries one such loop.

§01

Introduction

premise, threat model, how to read

A confidential-inference deployment releases some function of a private input: an activation crossing a trust boundary, a sentence embedding in a vector store, an obfuscated embedding table. The question for each released object is how much of the secret it leaks, and whether an attack-independent measure predicts how much an attacker recovers.

Threat model (public weights). The default anchor is WEIGHTS-PUB: the adversary knows the model weights and the embedding table, so norms, Gram matrices, and attention scores are known functions of the secret, and the adversary can synthesize unlimited training pairs at any defense parameter. Comparisons between admissible attacks are therefore fair. Per-page deviations are stated in that page.

Probe is not the attack. A probe is an independent measure that correlates with attack recovery, never the attack reporting its own bits. A measure that could not be computed without running the attack is the attack in disguise, and any correlation it shows is circular. The probes here are geometry-only or channel-matched: a capacity-matched token-identity reader, a spectral channel mutual-information ceiling, geometry-only error bounds.

One metric, two axes. Every measure reports on one comparable scale, bits, beside a per-secret human readout: token-identity perplexity and top-k, text token-F1 and ROUGE, permutation recovery-rate and rank correlation, embedding token-F1 and cosine, membership AUC. The reporting layer that enforces this convention is documented under the metric convention.

How to read this site. The cards below group by released surface: the residual stream, the pooled or per-position embedding, and the permuted embedding table. Each links a page that runs the loop end to end. Two cross-cutting pages follow: the reporting foundation every page shares, and a cryptographic zero-leakage reference that bounds the leakage scale from below.

Permutation cover

An embedding table is hidden by permuting feature columns and adding noise; the adversary must re-identify each obfuscated row.

secret
the row-to-token correspondence of an obfuscated embedding table
probe
attack-independent mutual information on the permutation-invariant signature
attack
full sorted-row matcher against a 64-bin quantile baseline
finding
the baseline collapses under small noise while the probe shows leakage persists; the full sorted row, the maximal invariant of the column permutation, recovers it, adding 0.60 recovery where the baseline has lost three quarters of its accuracy; the probe tracks the strong matcher across the sweep

§04

Zero-leakage reference

cryptographic anchor, documented not swept

The sweeps above measure leakage that varies with a privacy parameter. Cryptographic confidential-inference schemes anchor the opposite end of the scale: they leak essentially nothing about the protected surface by construction, at the cost of homomorphic-encryption, secure-multiparty-computation, or trusted-execution overhead. They have no privacy parameter to sweep, so they are documented here as a reference floor rather than measured as a tunable defense.

SchemeMechanism classProtected surfaceLeakage
Euston cryptographic confidential inference (encrypted computation) private input and intermediate activations ~0 by construction
Fision cryptographic confidential inference (encrypted computation) private input and intermediate activations ~0 by construction
TwinShield (full) trusted-execution and accelerator co-design with dual data-and-model protection; attention and softmax computed inside the trusted boundary input, activations, and model weights ~0 by construction

Read as a converse: any practical defense that releases a representation in the clear and tunes a privacy parameter sits above this floor, and the question this study answers for each is how far above, in bits, and whether that distance predicts what an attacker recovers. These schemes are reference-only because their cost moves the engineering question from how much leaks to whether the encryption or trusted-execution budget is affordable.
