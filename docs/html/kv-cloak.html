KV-Cloak
— channel decoupling

KV-Cloak obfuscates the stored key/value cache as a product of three operations: a secret orthogonal token mixing, a one-time random block permutation, and a secret orthogonal feature mixing, with an additive beacon mask. Its advertised security parameter is the block size, whose factorial permutation count is presented as the barrier. Measured against a key-free source-separation adversary on the raw keys, the three operations separate onto three independent observables, and only one of them provides protection. The secret feature mixing is the sole operation that drives reconstruction to chance; the permutation and the block size leave reconstruction plaintext-like and do not protect against this adversary over the block sizes tested. The block size provides no protection against this adversary.

research report · 2026-06-24
§01

Introduction

the defense, the threat model, the headline

KV-Cloak (arXiv 2508.09442, NDSS 2026) protects the key/value cache by exposing, per PagedAttention block of b tokens, the obfuscated key matrix K' = S · P̂ · (K + A) · M: S and per-head M are secret orthogonal matrices, P̂ is a fresh one-time-pad token permutation, and A is a structured beacon mask. The transform is invertible for the legitimate party and leaves the attention output unchanged.

The threat model is WEIGHTS-PUB: the adversary knows the weights and embeddings, reads the obfuscated cache K' from memory, but does not hold the secret matrices. The secret is the raw per-head key matrix K itself. Because the attention output is invariant under the defense, the leakage surface is the stored K', not the post-attention activations. The attacks are the source-separation family: single-observation independent component analysis and joint diagonalization across stacked observations, graded by the Hungarian-matched cosine of recovered sources to the plaintext key rows against a random-demixing chance baseline.

The headline is a threat-model correction. KV-Cloak argues that the block-wise permutation imposes a b! barrier, so larger blocks are more secure. That barrier binds an adversary who must recover the token order. A subspace-membership or component-analysis adversary never needs the order: it recovers a basis of the key-row subspace, which the permutation and the token mixing leave intact. The block size is therefore irrelevant to it, and reconstruction is controlled entirely by the secret feature mixing M.

§03

Method

surface, ablation, attacks, probe

The defense is implemented as a representation transform and applied to the raw per-head keys captured from Qwen3-4B at layers 0, 12, and 20 over 48 prompts of 110 to 178 tokens. The sweep crosses the channel ablation (feature mixing alone; token mixing with permutation; permutation alone; the linear form without permutation or mask; mask alone; the full transform) with block sizes 16, 32, 64, mask energies 0, 1, 4, and three random key draws, for 273 configurations.

The attacks are the joint-diagonalization separators run on the obfuscated keys; reconstruction is the 95th-percentile Hungarian-matched cosine to the plaintext key rows, graded against the random-demixing baseline. The probe is the whitened-row negentropy separability (J), a geometry-only measure of non-Gaussianity computed without running any separation, reported in bits. The shared spectral capacity is the accumulation-axis probe. Reconstruction is the per-secret readout; the probes are the bits canonical.

§06

Findings

which operation provides protection

The secret feature mixing is the only operation that protects against this adversary. It is a secret rotation of the per-head feature frame that the key-free adversary cannot align to the plaintext reference. It drives both the single-observation and the accumulation attack to the chance baseline and collapses the matched probe.

The permutation and the block size are inert. The token mixing and the permutation are invertible row operations: they preserve the key-row subspace, so the recovered directions and the chance baseline unchanged, and the matched probe plaintext-like in the sweep, and reconstruction stays far above baseline and accumulates with the observation count. Because the block size sizes only these operations, reconstruction is flat across 16, 32, and 64. The factorial permutation count defends an order-recovery adversary, not a subspace-membership adversary that never needs the token order.

The mask is the only channel that perturbs the Gram spectrum. Its energy is visible in the Gram-spectrum error, which rises from 0.24 to 4.70 as the mask energy grows, and it inflates the negentropy through its beacon outliers, but on its own it moves reconstruction only from 0.626 to 0.581 and does not drive it to chance.

§07

Theory

four lemmas, verified

The decoupling is exact. The feature mixing leaves the row-Gram invariant because it is orthogonal on the right (L1). The token mixing with permutation is an orthogonal similarity on the Gram, so its spectrum is preserved while the matrix is rotated, and the permutation alone preserves every permutation-symmetric statistic (L2). The token operators are left-invertible, so they preserve the key-row subspace exactly; the recoverable directions, the best-case reconstruction, and the chance baseline are therefore unchanged, for a fixed block (L3).

The feature mixing has a quantitative ceiling. A secret uniform orthogonal matrix rotates the key-row subspace to an independent random subspace, so the best alignment any key-free attack can reach against a fixed plaintext row is the projection of that row onto the rotated subspace. Its squared length follows a Beta law with mean s/d, where s is the block rank and d the head dimension. A Bernstein tail and a union bound over the n graded targets give an attack-independent ceiling on the expected reconstruction of order √(s/d) + √(log n / d), which reduces to √(s/d) when the target count is small relative to the rank and tends to the chance level as the head dimension grows relative to the rank (L4). With a head dimension of 128 and rank capped at 16, the ceiling is about 0.35, and the measured feature-mixing reconstruction (0.126) sits at the chance baseline (0.157). The lemmas passed cross-model verification; the proofs are in the claim file cited in the footer.

§08

Discussion

scope and consequence

The consequence for the defense is specific: against a subspace-membership or component-analysis adversary the block size is not a security parameter, and the security rests entirely on the secret feature mixing. This refines, rather than contradicts, the original analysis, which bounds an order-recovery adversary for whom the permutation does bind.

The scope is bounded. The reconstruction ceiling is an upper bound and is informative only when the block rank is small relative to the head dimension, the regime here. The block-size result is established over the standard sizes 16, 32, 64. The matched probe predicts reconstruction between channel families, not within one, and inverts under the additive mask. These are the honest limits of the present result; the proofs hold within them.
