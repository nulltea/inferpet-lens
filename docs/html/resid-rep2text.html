Rep2Text
— extraction, not capacity

Rep2Text trains a small adapter that maps a single last-token residual vector into soft-prompt embeddings for a frozen decoder, which then regenerates the input text. On the layer-10 residual of a 4-billion-parameter model, this attack recovers a statistically significant but small amount of input-specific information at every sequence length, and that amount does not fall as sequences grow longer. A single residual vector is therefore not a binding information bottleneck at this depth: its measured channel capacity of about 2856 bits far exceeds the entropy of even the longest prompts. As a result, a geometry-only capacity probe does not predict recovery across sequence length. Recovery here is limited by what a bounded decoder can extract, not by channel capacity.

§01

Introduction

the attack, the threat model, the headline

Rep2Text (arXiv 2511.06571) inverts a hidden representation back to text with an adapter-plus-decoder design: a trained multilayer perceptron maps one representation vector to a short sequence of soft-prompt embeddings, and a frozen pretrained language model decodes those embeddings autoregressively into a token sequence. Only the adapter is trained; the decoder weights are fixed.

Here the secret is the input text, the representation is the last-token residual stream after block 10 of Qwen3-4B (one 2560-dimensional vector per prompt), and the frozen decoder is Qwen3-1.7B, which shares the Qwen3 tokenizer so recovery is scored in a single token space. The threat model is that the adversary holds the public model and an auxiliary corpus on which to train the adapter, but not the target's private text. Recovery is graded by token-level F1 and ROUGE-L against the true input tokens; the privacy parameter swept is isotropic Gaussian noise added to the residual.

The question is the one this project asks of every surface: does an attack-independent information measure predict how well the attack recovers the secret? The candidate measure is the geometry-only spectral channel mutual information of the residual ensemble, already validated for sentence-embedding inversion. A single residual vector is a natural bottleneck candidate, since one fixed-dimensional vector must encode an entire variable-length prefix. The headline is that this bottleneck does not bind at layer 10: capacity exceeds the secret entropy by more than a factor of two, so the capacity measure is uninformative about recovery across sequence length, and the true constraint is extractability.

§02

Method

adapter, decoder, controls, probe

The adapter is a three-layer perceptron mapping the standardized 2560-dimensional residual to eight soft-prompt embeddings of the decoder. It is trained by teacher-forced next-token prediction through the frozen decoder for ten passes over 762 prompts; the cross-entropy falls from 3.07 to 2.09 nats. Recovery is evaluated by greedy generation from the soft prompts on 138 held-out prompts, stratified into six length buckets of 23 prompts each. The corpus is 1965 natural-language prompts drawn from question and instruction datasets, stratified to span 6 to 59 words.

Two controls subtract priors that are not residual-specific leakage. The mean-residual control feeds every test prompt the ensemble-mean vector; it recovers 0.002 token-F1, confirming the metric registers no per-prompt information when none is present. The shuffled-residual control feeds a different prompt's residual, averaged over five independent draws; it recovers about 0.108 token-F1, which is the frozen decoder's language and common-token prior. Genuine leakage is the difference between real and shuffled recovery, with paired bootstrap 95% confidence intervals over 5000 resamples.

The probe is the spectral channel mutual information I_G(σ) = ½ Σ log₂(1 + λ_i/σ²) of the residual covariance spectrum, evaluated at the injected noise level plus a fixed geometry-only reference floor. It is computed from the covariance and the noise level alone; it never trains a decoder, never reads target text, and cannot be computed by running the attack. The rate-distortion ceiling it assigns to a length-L secret is r(L) = min(I_G, H_X(L)) / H_X(L), where H_X(L) = L·h is the secret entropy under an upper per-token-entropy proxy.

§03

Results

recovery by length, capacity, the noise sweep

Recovery rises with sequence length rather than falling. Genuine residual-specific leakage is positive at every length and every bootstrap confidence interval excludes zero, so the leakage is real, but it is small: the largest gap is +0.089 token-F1 at 59 tokens, and most of the raw recovery is the decoder's prior. The leakage is largest for the longest prompts, the opposite of what a binding bottleneck would produce.

The measured capacity of the residual ensemble at the reference floor is 2856 bits. The longest bucket averages 59 tokens (about 1019 bits under the upper proxy), and the capped maximum length of 64 tokens is at most 1102 bits; a realistic per-token estimate gives 470 to 650 bits. Capacity therefore exceeds the secret entropy by more than a factor of two at every tested length. At the plaintext operating point the rate-distortion ceiling equals 1 for every length, so its length-only rank correlation with recovery is undefined (a constant predictor); across the full length-by-noise grid of 36 points it is 0.18.

Injecting noise to force capacity downward confirms the mechanism. Capacity falls from 2856 bits to 24 bits across the sweep while recovery falls only from 0.150 to 0.077; the genuine leakage gap stays flat at about +0.038 until more than 80% of capacity is removed, then collapses. The two quantities agree in rank order across the sweep, but the agreement is uninformative near the plaintext operating point: capacity must be largely destroyed before recovery responds.

§04

Theory

why a capacity probe cannot rank recovery when capacity is slack

The failure of the capacity probe to predict recovery across length is not an artifact of the data; it follows from a short lemma. Fix one residual covariance and hold it constant across lengths, so the spectral channel mutual information I_G(σ) is a function of the covariance and the noise level alone and is independent of the secret length. A length-L secret has positive entropy H_X(L) = L·h with per-token entropy h > 0, and the probe assigns the normalized throughput ceiling r(L) = min(I_G, H_X(L)) / H_X(L), used here only as a cross-length ranking proxy.

Capacity-slack vacuity. If capacity is slack over the tested range, that is I_G(σ) ≥ H_X(L_max), then for every tested length H_X(L) ≤ H_X(L_max) ≤ I_G, so min(I_G, H_X(L)) = H_X(L) and r(L) = 1 identically. For two or more lengths the proxy is then rank-constant, its sample variance is zero, and its Spearman correlation with any recovery profile is undefined; under the constant-input convention it is reported as zero, meaning no rank signal. No property of the recovery profile enters the argument, so the conclusion holds independent of the attack.

The proxy only begins to discriminate by length once L > I_G/h. With capacity 2856 bits and an upper per-token entropy of about 17.2 bits, this requires sequences longer than 167 tokens; under a realistic 8 to 11 bits per token the threshold rises to 260 to 358 tokens. Both are far beyond the 59-token maximum tested, which is exactly why the measured correlation is near zero. A stronger attack raises recovery toward, but never above, the saturated ceiling, so no increase in attack strength can make the capacity probe discriminate by length in this regime. The proof is verified by cross-model review.

§05

Discussion

what the matched probe must measure; scope

The result refines an earlier negative finding on the per-position residual stream, where an iterative embedding-feedback inverter gained nothing because that surface has no bottleneck. The single last-token vector was the natural place to expect a bottleneck to appear, and at layer 10 it does not: capacity is slack, recovery is limited by extractability, and a capacity measure is the wrong matched probe. The measure that should track recovery here is an extractable-information quantity computed under the decoder's hypothesis class, rather than channel capacity. Building that measure, alongside a stronger-attack ablation to separate attack weakness from representation limits, is the queued follow-up.

Scope. The findings are for one source and decoder pair, layer 10, a single trained adapter and seed, and 23 prompts per length bucket. The shuffled null is a permutation rather than a strict derangement, which makes the leakage estimate conservative rather than inflated; the entropy term is an upper proxy, which does not affect the length ranking. The capacity-slack lemma is general; the empirical refutation is scoped to this setup.
