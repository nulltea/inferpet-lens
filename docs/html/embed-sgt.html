Stained Glass embedding defense
— a valid mutual-information ceiling that does not rank noise shapes

A sentence-embedding inversion adversary observes a pooled embedding released under the Stained Glass Transform, which adds learned anisotropic Gaussian noise. A geometry-only channel mutual-information measure, computed without the attack, predicts text recovery perfectly as the noise scale rises within a single noise shape (rank correlation 1.0). Holding that measure fixed and varying only the noise shape breaks the prediction: at an identical 826.8 bits the recovered token-F1 spans 0.048 to 0.566, and across the twelve noisy settings the measure correlates with recovery at only 0.48, while released-embedding cosine correlates at 0.97. The measure is a correct converse on every shape but is blind to which directions the noise occupies, and the deployed inverter is governed by that occupancy. The reason is proved, not asserted.

research report · 2026-06-24
§01

Introduction

the defense, the threat model, the headline

A pooled sentence embedding is a compact, invertible summary of its input text. Releasing one to an untrusted server invites a reconstruction attack: a pretrained iterative corrector can recover much of the original text from the vector alone. The Stained Glass Transform (arXiv 2506.09452) defends the release by adding learned heteroscedastic Gaussian noise, with a training loss that caps the mutual information between the clean and released vectors. The defense therefore claims an information budget, and the natural question is whether that budget predicts how much text the attack recovers.

A companion study already established that a geometry-only channel mutual-information measure is a complete predictor of this attack under isotropic noise: across a privacy sweep it tracks recovery at rank correlation 1.0. The Stained Glass Transform differs from isotropic noise in exactly one respect, the noise shape, which is anisotropic by design. This isolates a clean test. Construct several noise shapes that all spend the same number of bits, and ask whether recovery is the same across them. If it is, the scalar budget is a complete matched predictor. If it is not, the budget is a valid ceiling but not a predictor, and the result identifies what the attack actually responds to.

The answer is that recovery is not the same. At a matched budget of 826.8 bits the recovered token-F1 is 0.048 for an adversarial tail-loaded shape, 0.433 for isotropic noise, and 0.566 for the utility-optimal shape the defense itself selects. Across the twelve noisy settings the budget correlates with recovery at 0.48, below the 0.6 threshold, while the cosine between the clean and released embeddings correlates at 0.97 and the total noise energy at 0.95. The budget is shape-blind. This page reports the sweep and proves why a correct mutual-information converse fails to order the noise shapes a fixed inverter actually distinguishes.

measure (bits)Gaussian channel-capacity MI ceiling of the Gaussian release channel, computed from the embedding covariance and the noise covariance alone, with no attack

The measure is the Gaussian-channel mutual information between the clean and released vectors, $I_G=\tfrac12\log_2\det(I+D^{-1/2}\Sigma D^{-1/2})$, where $\Sigma$ is the embedding covariance and $D$ the diagonal noise covariance. It is a closed-form function of the two covariances and never sees the attack, so it qualifies as an attack-independent measure rather than the attack reporting its own success. It generalizes the isotropic-noise measure of the companion study to an arbitrary diagonal noise covariance.

§03

Matched-budget noise shapes

three allocations of the same number of bits

Work in the basis where the embedding covariance is diagonal with eigenvalues $\lambda_1\ge\dots\ge\lambda_d$, the principal directions of the embedding ranked by signal energy. A noise shape assigns a variance $v_i$ to each principal direction, and the budget is $I_G=\tfrac12\sum_i\log_2(1+\lambda_i/v_i)$. The total distortion, equal to the expected squared noise norm, is $\sum_i v_i$. For each target budget the sweep constructs three shapes by bisection, all hitting the same budget to within numerical precision:

isotropicequal variance on every direction; the noise model of the companion isotropic study
utility-optimalthe variance allocation that minimizes total distortion at the fixed budget, computed in closed form by reverse water-filling; this is the allocation the Stained Glass training loss converges to
tail-loadedan adversarial shape that places its noise on the low-energy tail directions, spending almost no budget there while injecting large total distortion; a worst case at matched budget

The budget targets are 826.8, 434.1, 196.0, and 71.4 bits, spanning the range of the companion study, plus the clean embedding with only a fixed-norm clip applied. Each of the twelve noisy settings and the clean setting is inverted on the same 96 held-out texts. The construction is verified independently: across the three shapes at each budget the measure agrees to within $2.3\times10^{-13}$ bits, so any difference in recovery is a difference between equally-informative releases, not a difference in the budget.

§05

Findings

a complete predictor within a shape, blind across shapes

Within a fixed noise shape the budget is a perfect predictor. Down the isotropic and utility-optimal columns the rank correlation between budget and recovery is 1.0. This reproduces the isotropic result of the companion study and confirms that nothing about the generalized measure or the attack is broken.

Across noise shapes at a matched budget the prediction fails. At 826.8 bits the recovered token-F1 ranges from 0.048 to 0.566, and across the twelve noisy settings the budget correlates with recovery at 0.48, below the 0.6 threshold (including the clean anchor as infinite budget moves this only to 0.59). Restricting the budget to the top signal directions does not repair it (0.19). What does correlate is the released-embedding cosine (0.97) and the negative total distortion (0.95): recovery is governed by how close the released vector stays to the clean one, not by the number of bits.

The utility-optimal defense is also the most invertible. The shape the Stained Glass loss selects has lower total distortion and higher released cosine than isotropic noise at every budget, which is the privacy-utility advantage the defense claims. The same property makes it the easiest to invert: it recovers at 0.566 against 0.433 for isotropic noise at 826.8 bits. Minimizing distortion at a fixed budget keeps the directions the inverter reads clean, so the utility the defense preserves is utility for the attacker as well.

§06

Why a valid ceiling is not a predictor

three lemmas, verified

The gap is explained, not merely observed. Three lemmas separate what the measure proves from what it cannot. The first two are independent of any model of the attack; the third uses one explicit assumption about the inverter.

Lemma 1: the measure is a valid converse and falls monotonically within a shape. For the Gaussian release channel the measure equals the mutual information between the clean and released vectors, and for a non-Gaussian embedding with the same covariance it is an upper bound, because the Gaussian maximizes entropy at fixed covariance. Treating the input text as the discrete secret and chaining text to clean embedding to released embedding, the data-processing inequality and Fano's inequality bound the exact-reconstruction error from below by a quantity that grows as the budget falls. Along a single shape scaled by an overall noise level, the budget is strictly decreasing, so the ceiling falls monotonically. This is the ceiling the within-shape correlation of 1.0 tracks.

Lemma 2: the measure cannot see which directions carry the noise. The set of noise covariances that spend a fixed budget is a surface of one less dimension than the space of covariances, and over that surface the total distortion ranges from a finite minimum to unbounded. The minimum is the reverse water-filling allocation, which is the utility-optimal shape; the unbounded direction is the tail-loaded family, which meets the budget on tail directions while its total distortion grows without limit. The measure depends only on the ratios of signal energy to noise energy per direction, so two allocations that place the same noise on different directions have the same budget. A scalar that is constant across these allocations cannot order them by anything that depends on which directions are noisy. This is the structural reason the budget is shape-blind, and it holds for any inverter.

Lemma 3: a distance-bounded inverter responds to distortion, not to the budget. Model the fixed inverter as responding to the cosine distance of the released vector: its recovery degradation is bounded by a constant times the expected cosine distortion. That distortion is in turn bounded by a constant times the total noise energy. At a fixed budget the utility-optimal shape minimizes total energy, so it minimizes the recovery-loss ceiling, while the tail-loaded shape drives the energy and the cosine loss to their extremes. The predictor matched to this inverter is therefore the total distortion or the released cosine, which the data confirm at rank correlations of 0.95 and 0.97. This step rests on the distance-bounded inverter assumption, stated explicitly and corroborated by the 0.97 correlation rather than derived from the attack internals.

All three lemmas were verified by an independent adversarial proof review across two rounds, with the Fano step restricted to exact reconstruction, the minimizer established by a convex reformulation, the cosine bound corrected to a global constant, and the headline restricted to the two claims the proof supports. The full proof is recorded with the claim.

§07

Discussion

what is settled, what is open

The measure is a correct ceiling and a perfect within-shape predictor, and it is blind to the noise shape. The blindness is structural: a single number summarizing the per-direction signal-to-noise ratios cannot encode where the noise sits, and the deployed inverter is governed by exactly that. The matched predictor for this attack is the released cosine or the total distortion, which is the right quantity to report alongside the budget when assessing a Stained Glass defense.

The scope is deliberately narrow. The study covers one released surface, one frozen encoder, and one pretrained inverter held fixed across all settings, at 96 held-out texts per setting and a single noise realization per cell. The reported recoveries and rank correlations are point estimates without resampling intervals, and the tail-loaded shape is an extreme matched-budget construction chosen as a worst case rather than a continuum. These do not affect the structural result, which is a statement about the measure and holds for any inverter, but they bound the empirical claims to this surface and this attack.

One question remains open. The tail-loaded shape preserves the budget, which means the information the measure certifies is still present, retained in the low-energy directions the fixed inverter does not read. A shape-aware inverter, one that whitens the released vector by the known noise covariance before correcting or that is retrained per shape, might recover that information and restore the correlation with the budget. Whether such an inverter exists separates two readings of the result: that the measure is the wrong predictor for this fixed attack, which is proved, or that the attack is too weak to reach the certified ceiling, which is not settled here. The next study builds a read-subspace measure matched to the inverter and a shape-aware inverter to decide between them.
