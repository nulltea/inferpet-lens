KV/QKV
— source separation

Can a blind-source-separation adversary recover the activation rows behind the key/query/value and residual surfaces? On the plaintext surface the answer is no: with no hidden mixing, every candidate demixing returns a basis of one fixed subspace, so the Hungarian-cosine recovery score is dominated by subspace membership, not by separation. Once recovery is graded against the matched random-demixing floor, the genuine margin is near zero and does not grow with the number of observations. A geometry-only whitened-row negentropy separability (J) probe predicts that genuine margin, and only that margin. This is the baseline against which the mixing defenses are measured.

research report · 2026-06-24
§01

Introduction

the question, the threat model, the headline

Blind source separation (BSS) recovers unknown sources from their observed mixture when the mixing matrix is unknown. Ported to a transformer, the question is whether an adversary holding the key/query/value products or the residual stream can separate the per-token activation rows that produced them.

The threat model is WEIGHTS-PUB: the adversary knows the weights and embeddings, observes the surface in the clear, and runs the joint-diagonalization family of separators (single-observation JADE; joint diagonalization across stacked observations). The secret is the set of activation rows behind kq, kqv_out, and resid_post.

The headline finding is negative and exact. On the plaintext surface the effective mixing is the identity: the observed operand equals its own source rows. There is no hidden mixing to invert, so there are no concealed sources to recover, and the recovery score reported by the attack measures something else entirely. Graded against the matched null, the genuine separation margin has median 0.027 (on a cosine scale where the raw score reads 0.776), and its slope in the number of stacked observations is +0.009 per doubling, i.e. flat. The matched negentropy probe tracks the genuine margin at Spearman 0.92 across the kind-by-layer cells, while it anticorrelates (−0.43) with the uncorrected raw score. This page is the plaintext reference point; the same attacks and probe are re-run under the mixing defenses, where any rise in recovery is attributable to the defense, not to separation of the plaintext.

§03

Method

surface → separation attack → matched probe → floor-corrected margin
FIG. 01 — operand → whitening → demixing (attack vs matched null) → floor-corrected margin · probe runs in parallel without the attack navy = observed surface · slate = adversary separation · plum = matched null · amber = reported margin

The separation pipeline. For each prompt the attack forms the operand, centers and whitens it so the rows are uncorrelated and unit-scaled, then applies the joint-diagonalization rotation that the separator selects from the whitened fourth-order cumulants. The recovered rows are scored against the true rows by the Hungarian-aligned cosine, reported at the 95th percentile. The companion joint-diagonalization attack repeats this across a stack of observations to test for accumulation.

The matched null is the load-bearing step. Under the identity transform the whitened rows already span the operand's own row space, so any rotation, whether the separator's or a random one, returns a basis of the same fixed subspace. The recovery score is therefore high for reasons that have nothing to do with finding the right rotation. The matched control replaces the separator's rotation with a uniformly random one, holding the whitened data and the alignment step fixed; the genuine separation is the recovery above this floor. Grading instead against an unrelated-Gaussian target measures a different quantity and inflates the apparent recovery.

The probe runs in parallel and never calls the separator. The negentropy probe is a function of the whitened-row moments only; it could be computed with the attack code deleted. This is what lets it serve as an attack-independent predictor rather than the attack reporting its own score.

§04

Leakage Measures

why each probe is attack-independent, what it bounds
Whitened-row negentropy separability (J)The summed Hyvärinen negentropy of the whitened rows, a fourth-moment separability surrogate related to the structure the single-observation separator exploits. It measures how far the rows are from Gaussian, hence how separable they are by independent component analysis. Computed from row skewness and excess kurtosis only; it does not run joint diagonalization, so it is independent of the separation attack.
Shared spectral capacity (bits)The Gaussian water-filling capacity of the averaged row covariance over a stack of observations, the structure the joint-diagonalization attack accumulates. It mirrors the attack's stacking but computes only an eigenspectrum and a capacity, not a demixing. The accumulation-axis companion probe.
Matched floor (cosine)Not a probe but the grading null: the expected Hungarian-cosine of a random rotation against the true rows. Holding it fixed, the genuine separation is recovery above this floor. Established analytically (every demixing returns a basis of one fixed subspace) and measured at 0.708.

§06

Findings

what the plaintext surface supports

The separation attack is ill-posed on the plaintext surface. With no hidden mixing, every candidate demixing returns a basis of one fixed subspace, so the recovery score is a measure of subspace membership and not of separation. The matched random-demixing floor is the correct grading null. Against it the genuine margin (0.027) is only about 3.4% of the raw score; the remaining ≈ 96% is membership baseline. The unrelated-Gaussian floor measures a different quantity and under-counts the membership baseline by 0.553 (about 71% of the raw score). This is a structural fact, proved in the analysis below; the magnitudes are measured.

Recovery does not accumulate with observations. The slope in the number of stacked observations is flat. Joint diagonalization across observations is informative only when a single fixed unknown mixing is shared across them; under the identity transform there is no such mixing for the stack to constrain. The flat slope is consistent with this and was confirmed empirically.

The matched probe predicts the genuine margin, and only the genuine margin. The negentropy probe orders the kind-by-layer cells in agreement with the floor-corrected margin at Spearman 0.92, with a permutation p-value of 0.0013, while it anticorrelates with the uncorrected score. The result is exploratory: it rests on nine cells and is carried by the ordering across the three surface kinds rather than by resolution within a kind. It establishes that the choice of grading null is what makes or breaks the predictor.

§07

Analysis

the subspace-membership floor; proof sketch

Subspace-membership floor. Assume the centered operand has full row rank and at least twice as many columns as rows. Whitening produces rows that are orthonormal under the sample inner product. For any invertible left-multiplication, the rows of the product are fixed linear combinations of the operand rows, so they span exactly the operand's row space; the converse holds because the multiplication is invertible. Hence every demixing output, whether the separator's rotation or a random one, spans the same fixed subspace.

Because a whitened basis stays orthonormal under any orthogonal rotation, varying the rotation only rotates one fixed orthonormal basis within that fixed subspace. The recovery score is therefore a function of the fixed subspace and the fixed true rows, evaluated along a rotation orbit, and its average over uniformly random rotations is a fixed baseline that does not depend on whether the separator succeeded. The unrelated-Gaussian control instead replaces the true rows by random targets that, with probability one, lie outside the subspace; it measures a different functional. The only rotation-dependent quantity is recovery above the random-rotation baseline, which is the reported margin. The size and sign of the gap between the two grading nulls are not fixed by this argument; they are measured (0.708 against 0.155).

This argument is a structural invariance and a statement about which null is matched; it does not bound the baseline in closed form. An attempt to lower-bound the baseline analytically did not survive review and is not claimed. The flat accumulation slope is reported as a measurement supported by the identifiability argument above, not as a theorem. The full statement and proof are in the claim file cited in the footer.

§08

Discussion

what leaks, the role of this baseline, limitations

What leaks. On the plaintext surface, essentially nothing beyond subspace membership, which is not separation. The residual stream shows the largest genuine margin (0.06 to 0.07) and the keys-times-queries product the smallest (near zero), an ordering the negentropy probe reproduces.

Why this baseline matters. A mixing-defense sweep, key/value cloaking or residual mixing, is the next place where these separation attacks could become informative; this plaintext baseline makes those sweeps interpretable. Because the plaintext margin is near zero and flat, any rise in recovery observed under a mixing defense would be attributable to the defense's mixing or mask rather than to separation of the underlying plaintext. This page fixes that reference point and supplies the matched grading null the defense sweeps reuse.

Limitations. The probe-vs-margin correlation rests on nine cells and resolves surface kinds rather than layers within a kind; it is reported as exploratory with its permutation test. The accumulation slope is measured on observation counts up to eight (sixteen was unavailable at this corpus size). The qualitative result, that separation is ill-posed on plaintext and the matched probe tracks the small genuine margin, is settled at this scope; a larger corpus would tighten the slope interval on a flat line without changing the conclusion, so the heavier run was not made.
