Depth inversion
— recovery persists with depth

A token-recovery attack on the residual stream of a 4-billion-parameter model recovers input-token identity at every sampled depth, from the first block to the last, with no collapse in deep layers. A learned nonlinear inverter exceeds a linear baseline at the deepest layer. Two attack-independent information measures, a capacity-matched token-identity reader and a mutual-information upper bound, track recovery across depth, so an attack-free measure predicts how far the attack succeeds.

research report · 2026-06-24
§01

Introduction

the attack, the threat model, the headline

A common intuition holds that deep internal states of a language model are safe to expose because successive nonlinear blocks scramble the input beyond recovery. Dong et al. (arXiv 2507.16372) challenge this for full hidden states. This report tests the residual stream of Qwen3-4B across depth and asks the question this project asks of every surface: does an attack-independent information measure predict how well an inversion attack recovers the secret?

The secret is the input-token identity at each position, the surface is the residual activation after a transformer block, and the threat model is that the adversary holds the public model weights and embedding table but not the target text. An inverter maps a residual vector to a predicted token by cosine match against the public embedding table. Recovery is the top-1 retrieval rate after subtracting a label-shuffle floor, so only generalizing recovery counts. The train and test token vocabularies are disjoint, which removes any credit from memorizing seen tokens.

The headline has three parts. Recovery does not vanish with depth: the best inverter holds a shuffle-subtracted selectivity between 0.39 and 0.69 at all nine sampled depths, and every confidence interval excludes zero. A learned two-layer inverter beats a linear ridge inverter at the deepest layer, where the ridge alone would understate the leak. And the capacity-matched token-identity reader tracks recovery across depth at a rank correlation of +0.85, with a mutual-information upper bound tracking at +0.78, placing this surface in the regime where an attack-independent measure predicts the attack.

§03

Method

capture, inverters, controls, probes

Residual activations were captured for 512 prompts at nine depths, giving 9469 token positions per layer. Positions were split by token vocabulary into disjoint train and test sets, 3373 training and 413 test rows per layer, so a memorizing inverter cannot win on seen tokens. Three inverters were fit per layer: a cosine nearest-neighbour baseline that scores by direct match to the nearest training activation, a linear ridge map from residual to embedding, and a learned two-layer perceptron. Each predicted embedding was matched by cosine similarity to the public embedding table, and top-1 retrieval was scored against the true token.

The label-shuffle control permutes the position-to-token pairing, breaking the link between activation and token and leaving only the frequency prior. Its accuracy is the floor, and reported recovery is real minus shuffle. The nearest-neighbour baseline measures pure memorization and is zero by construction under the vocabulary-disjoint split. Two attack-independent measures were computed on the same activations: a capacity-matched reader that classifies token identity from the residual using token classes rather than the embedding table the attack retrieves against, and a contrastive mutual-information upper bound between residual and embedding. Neither measure runs the attack or reads the retrieval target, so any agreement with recovery is not mechanical.

The surface is one residual vector at depth ℓ. The attack branch (solid) maps it to a token by retrieval against the public embedding table and scores shuffle-subtracted selectivity. The probe branch (dashed) reads token-identity information from the same vector without the embedding table or the attack, and reports reader accuracy and a rank-only mutual-information upper-bound score. The measurement loop asks whether the two branches agree across depth.
§04

Leakage measures

why they are attack-independent

The capacity-matched reader classifies token identity from the residual through a fixed-width reduction followed by a softmax over token classes. It reads token classes, never the embedding table that the inverter retrieves against, and its dimension budget is matched so the reported accuracy is comparable across depths. Its label-shuffle floor is 0.001 to 0.005 at every depth, confirming the reader carries no label leakage of its own. The mutual-information upper bound is a contrastive estimator between residual and embedding; it estimates a bound on shared information and does not optimize the retrieval objective. Because neither quantity can be computed by running the attack, a correlation between either measure and recovery is evidence that the measure predicts the attack, not that it restates it.

Two scope notes keep the independence honest. The reader uses a row split over a shared, capped set of frequent token classes rather than the attack's vocabulary-disjoint split, so its accuracy and the attack's selectivity are not computed on identical partitions. The mutual-information bound is an estimator in embedding space, separate from retrieval but adjacent to the attack's target, and its absolute bit values are an upper-bound score read for rank order across depth, not a token-identity leakage count. The token-identity bit certificate comes only from the Fano lemma in the analysis.

Results

recovery by depth, the deepest-layer gap, measure tracking

Recovery is flat and non-monotone across depth and never collapses. The best inverter holds selectivity between 0.39 and 0.69 at all nine depths, and every interval excludes zero, with the lowest at the deepest layer. The memorization baseline is exactly zero everywhere, so the recovery is generalizing token inversion, not recall of seen tokens.

The learned and linear inverters are close through the middle of the network, but at the deepest layer the learned inverter reaches 0.542 while the linear inverter falls to 0.390, a gap of 0.153, and their confidence intervals are disjoint. Elsewhere the two overlap. The linear inverter's late-layer drop is therefore an artifact of the linear map, not a property of the representation: a nonlinear inverter recovers what the linear one misses at the final layer.

Both attack-independent measures move with recovery across depth. The capacity-matched reader accuracy tracks the best inverter at a rank correlation of +0.85, and tracks the linear and learned inverters separately at +0.80 and +0.83. The mutual-information upper bound tracks the best inverter at +0.78. With nine depths, a rank correlation of +0.85 has a permutation p-value below 0.01.

§06

Findings

claims, grounds, scope

Depth does not confer privacy on this residual stream (Supported). The best inverter's vocabulary-disjoint selectivity stays between 0.39 and 0.69 at every sampled depth, every interval excludes zero, and the memorization floor is zero, so the recovery is genuine and survives to the deepest layer. This reproduces the hidden-state finding of Dong et al. (arXiv 2507.16372) on Qwen3-4B. Scope: one model, one corpus, one seed, and the intervals are over test rows, not seeds.

A learned inverter beats a linear one at the deepest layer (Supported). At the final layer the learned inverter reaches 0.542 against the linear 0.390 with disjoint intervals; the two are comparable elsewhere. A study that used only a linear inverter would report a late-layer privacy gain that does not exist. Scope: single seed, so the gap is not yet robust to seed variation.

An attack-independent measure predicts recovery across depth (Supported). The capacity-matched reader tracks recovery at +0.85 and the mutual-information bound at +0.78 across the nine depths, with negligible label leakage in the reader. This is the positive regime of the measurement loop: a measure that cannot run the attack still ranks the attack's success by depth. Scope: single seed, nine depth points.

§07

Analysis

the certificate that turns recovery into information

Recovery does more than describe the surface; it bounds information. Treat the inverter as an estimator of the token from the residual. Because the inverter is fixed before it sees a test token, the token, the residual, and the prediction form a Markov chain, and Fano's inequality bounds the conditional entropy of the token given the residual by a function of the retrieval error and the pool size. Rearranged, the raw retrieval accuracy gives a lower bound on the mutual information between residual and token: I(token; residual) ≥ H(token) − H_b(1 − a) − (1 − a)·log(K − 1), where a is the retrieval accuracy, K the candidate-pool size, and H_b the binary entropy.

This bound is strictly positive once the raw accuracy exceeds a threshold that sits strictly above the chance rate 1/K, because Fano's bound is slack at chance. The two roles are distinct. The shuffle-subtracted selectivity does the operational work: the vocabulary-disjoint split removes the memorization route to accuracy and the label-shuffle control removes the frequency route, so a positive selectivity attributes signal to the residual itself and shows that a depth at which selectivity stays bounded away from zero cannot be a depth at which the residual is irreversible to the token, which holds at every sampled depth here. The positive bound in bits at each depth is the stronger statement; it uses the raw accuracy and additionally requires the candidate-pool size and a lower bound on the token entropy, and that per-depth bit count is a remaining follow-up. The lemma, including its corrected threshold and conservative finite-sample form, is stated and proved in full alongside this report.

§08

Discussion

contrast with noise, scope, follow-up

On plaintext residuals the attack-independent measure tracks recovery across depth. This is the opposite of the behaviour under input-layer differential privacy, where the same family of measures decouples from the attack as noise propagates deeper and the rank correlation even reverses sign at depth 20. The two results are complementary: depth alone does not hurt the measure-attack link, but propagated input noise does, because noise reshapes embedding geometry before it erases token identity. The deepest-layer result also resolves an earlier open question, where a trained perceptron lost to a linear inverter at depth under noise. On plaintext depth the learned inverter is at least competitive and wins at the final layer.

Scope. The findings are for one model, one corpus, and one seed, with intervals over test rows rather than seeds, and the depth grid samples every fourth block. The white-box per-sample optimization attack of Dong et al. is out of scope for this per-position retrieval pipeline. The learned inverter is the tractable amortized stand-in and already exceeds the linear baseline at depth. The certificate lemma is general. Converting it into a per-depth bit count, replicating across seeds and models, and adding a stronger inverter are the remaining follow-ups.
